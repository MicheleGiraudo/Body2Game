What is multiple sclerosis?
Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic neurological disease that affects the central nervous system, meaning the brain and spinal cord. In simple terms, MS is an autoimmune disease in which the immune system mistakenly attacks the myelin, the substance that covers and protects the nerve fibers. This damage creates scars (sclerosis) and disrupts communication between the brain and other parts of the body.
There are different forms of multiple sclerosis:
- Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS): This is the most common form of multiple sclerosis, characterized by periods of symptom exacerbation (relapses) followed by periods of remission, during which the symptoms may decrease or disappear completely.
- Secondary Progressive MS (SPMS): This form of multiple sclerosis initially develops as relapsing-remitting MS, but over time progresses into a progressive phase.
- Primary Progressive MS (PPMS): This form of multiple sclerosis is less common and manifests as a steady progression of symptoms from the start, without obvious periods of remission.
The diagnosis of multiple sclerosis is a complex process that requires a combination of different methodologies to obtain a clear and accurate clinical picture. This process includes a series of clinical examinations, advanced imaging techniques, and specific laboratory tests:
- Clinical Examinations
- Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
- Cerebrospinal Fluid Analysis
- Evoked Potentials
Among the most common symptoms are:
- Fatigue
- Balance and coordination problems
- Muscle weakness
- Visual disturbances
- Cognitive difficulties
In addition to these main symptoms, multiple sclerosis can present with a wide range of other signs and symptoms, including muscle spasms, pain, numbness, speech problems, mood disorders such as depression and anxiety, and difficulties in controlling the bladder and bowel.
Treatment: There is no definitive cure for MS, but there are several therapies that can help manage symptoms and slow the disease progression. These include disease-modifying drugs, symptomatic treatments, and rehabilitative therapies.
